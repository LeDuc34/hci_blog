LECTURE 10: The Shape of Smartphones Link to heading

The Evolution Link to heading

Early Era (2000-2007): Physical keyboards dominated. Think BlackBerry and Nokia - thick devices with tiny screens because typing mattered more than viewing.

iPhone Revolution (2007-2010): The shift to full touchscreen killed the keyboard. The 3.5-inch slab design became standard as content consumption replaced communication as the primary use.

Screen Size Wars (2010-2017): Displays grew from 4" to 6"+. Devices got thinner. Video streaming and social media demanded bigger screens.

Bezel Elimination (2017-Present): Edge-to-edge displays with notches and punch-holes maximized screen real estate. Glass-sandwich designs became universal.

Why Shapes Changed Link to heading

  • Technology improved: Better batteries, efficient processors, flexible displays
  • User behavior shifted: From communication device to multimedia consumption tool
  • Manufacturing evolved: Better materials, precision engineering
  • Market pressure: Differentiation through design
  • Ergonomics: Balancing screen size with usability

What’s Next? Link to heading

My prediction: Adaptive form factors through foldables and flexible displays.

Why this makes sense:

  1. Technology is ready: Foldables exist (Samsung Fold, Huawei Mate X) but expensive. Prices will drop as manufacturing scales.

  2. Solves the paradox: People want large screens AND portable devices. Foldables deliver both.

  3. Multiple use cases: Pocket-sized when folded, tablet-sized when open.

  4. Material science progress: Ultra-thin glass, flexible OLED, and durable hinges improve rapidly.

Long-term (10-15 years):

  • Rollable screens that extend from pen-sized devices
  • Wearable computing integrated into glasses/clothing
  • Shape-shifting materials that physically morph based on use
  • Ambient computing interfaces using any surface as a display through AR

LECTURE 11: The Computer for the 21st Century Link to heading

Weiser’s Vision (1991) Link to heading

Mark Weiser imagined a world where “specialized elements of hardware and software will be so ubiquitous that no one will notice their presence.”

Core Concepts:

Embodied Virtuality: Bring computing into the physical world, not force humans into virtual worlds.

Three Device Types:

  • Tabs (inch-scale): Wearable devices, hundreds per room
  • Pads (foot-scale): Paper-like computers you grab and use anywhere, dozens per room
  • Boards (yard-scale): Wall-sized displays, one or two per room

Three Waves of Computing:

  1. Many people, one computer (mainframe era)
  2. One person, one computer (PC era - “uneasy symbiosis”)
  3. Many computers, one person (ubiquitous era)

The Philosophy: Computers should vanish into the background and be “as refreshing as taking a walk in the woods” - not frustrating and attention-demanding.

Are We There Yet? Link to heading

‚úÖ What We’ve Achieved:

  1. The devices exist: Smartwatches (tabs), smartphones/tablets (pads), smart TVs (boards)
  2. Universal connectivity: Wi-Fi and 5G everywhere
  3. Location awareness: GPS in every pocket
  4. IoT everywhere: Smart homes, voice assistants, connected appliances
  5. Invisible functions: Contactless payments, cloud sync, automatic updates
  6. Multiple computers per person: We interact with 10+ devices daily

‚ùå What We’re Missing:

  1. SCREENS DEMAND TOO MUCH ATTENTION

    • Weiser wanted calm, invisible computing
    • We got attention-extraction machines designed for “engagement”
    • More trapped by screens than ever (social media, notifications)
    • Technology is NOT calm

  2. PRIVACY NIGHTMARE

    • Weiser warned: “hundreds of computers sensing people…have the potential to make totalitarianism seem sheerest anarchy”
    • This happened through corporate surveillance capitalism
    • Data breaches, tracking, loss of control
    • Can’t opt out without losing functionality

  3. NO INTEROPERABILITY

    • Apple vs. Google vs. Amazon walled gardens
    • Can’t “grab any pad” like Weiser envisioned
    • Fragmented standards, manual setup required

  4. TECHNOLOGY IS STILL VISIBLE

    • Constant charging, updating, managing
    • Notifications interrupt constantly
    • Error messages break the experience

  5. FAILED THE “WALK IN WOODS” TEST

    • Should reduce information overload - instead increased it
    • Should be calming - instead causes anxiety
    • Should disappear - instead dominates attention

The Fundamental Irony Link to heading

We built the technical infrastructure (devices, networks, sensors) but inverted the philosophy:

Weiser’s Vision What We Got
Calm technology Attention economy
Serves individuals Individuals serve platforms
Human-centered Algorithm-centered
Invisible computing Unavoidable screens
Reduces overload Creates overload

What Needs to Happen Link to heading

Technical:

  • Energy harvesting (no charging)
  • True edge computing (local processing)
  • Universal open standards
  • Natural interfaces (not screen-based)

Social/Policy:

  • Privacy by design
  • Data ownership rights
  • Interoperability mandates
  • Regulation of manipulative design
  • Ethical AI frameworks

Philosophical Shift:

  • From “engagement” to “enablement”
  • From “personalization” to “privacy”
  • From “smart” to “calm”
  • Measure success by how quickly users accomplish goals, not time-on-platform

Conclusion Link to heading

We’re living in ubiquitous computing without the calm.

We have the computers everywhere, but not the “refreshing walk in the woods” feeling. We got the devices but lost the vision. Weiser’s paper wasn’t just a technical prediction‚Äîit was a moral vision for how technology should enhance human life.

LECTURE 12: Most Downloaded MR Apps (Future) Link to heading

When MR headsets are as common as smartphones, what apps will dominate? Not “killer apps” that drive adoption, but everyday essentials.

Top 3 Most Downloaded Link to heading

1. Universal Spatial Workspace Link to heading

What: Complete productivity environment replacing physical monitors.

Features:

  • Infinite virtual screens anywhere in space
  • Persistent setups per location (home, coffee shop, office)
  • Holographic colleague presence
  • Gesture-based window management

Why #1:

  • Replaces expensive hardware: No $1000+ monitors needed
  • Work from anywhere: Full setup wherever you are
  • Universal need: Everyone who works digitally needs this
  • Clear ROI: Obvious productivity and cost benefits
  • Network effects: As companies adopt, individuals must follow

Like email on smartphones - fundamental, not optional.

2. Live Language & Cultural Overlay Link to heading

What: Real-time translation and cultural context for the physical world.

Features:

  • Instant translation of all visible text (signs, menus, documents)
  • Live speech translation with suggested responses
  • Cultural etiquette guidance (when to bow, gift-giving norms)
  • Historical context about locations, buildings, art
  • Social cues (formality preferences)
  • Accessibility features (subtitles, audio descriptions)

Why Top 3:

  • Global utility: Useful for travelers, multicultural environments
  • Removes barriers: Language is no longer an obstacle
  • Safety: Navigate confidently in unfamiliar places
  • Educational: Passive language learning
  • Inclusivity: Makes world accessible to people with disabilities

Like Google Translate, but seamlessly integrated into vision rather than requiring phone lookup.

3. Spatial Memory & Navigation Assistant Link to heading

What: AI that remembers where everything is and helps you never lose anything.

Features:

  • Object tracking: “Where are my keys?” ‚Üí AR arrow points
  • Spatial memory: Where you parked, where items are stored
  • Social memory: Face recognition with context (“You met John at Sarah’s party”)
  • Smart reminders: “Buy milk” appears when near grocery store
  • Route optimization: Best path through IKEA/airport
  • Attention direction: Highlights important things you might miss
  • Breadcrumb trails: Visual path in unfamiliar buildings

Why Top 3:

  • Universal frustration: Everyone loses things
  • Time savings: Average person spends 2.5 days/year looking for lost items
  • Cognitive augmentation: Extends human memory naturally
  • Immediate value: Useful from first use
  • Accessibility: Crucial for elderly, memory conditions
  • Safety: Emergency exits, escape routes

Like “Find My” but for everything, seamlessly integrated into daily life.

Why These Three? Link to heading

They share characteristics ensuring mass adoption:

  • ‚úì Universal applicability - everyone needs these
  • ‚úì Daily utility - used multiple times per day
  • ‚úì Clear value proposition - solve obvious problems
  • ‚úì Cost savings - replace expensive hardware or save time
  • ‚úì Low friction - enhance existing activities
  • ‚úì Network effects - more valuable as adoption grows
  • ‚úì Accessibility - help people with disabilities

The Pattern Link to heading

Just like smartphones, the most downloaded apps make MR indispensable rather than entertaining. Productivity, communication, and utility dominate entertainment in sustained usage.

The apps that win are those that become so useful you can’t imagine life without them‚Äînot the flashiest demos.
